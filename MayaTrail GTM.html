01 Positioning: graded drill, not continuous validation.

The cloud attack-emulation category exists - Mitigant proved that. The question is not whether buyers want to test detections; it is what shape they want to buy. Mitigant sells continuous adversarial exposure validation: an always-on agent-style integration with the SIEM. We do not compete on that framing.

MayaTrail sells the graded drill: a punctuated, evidentiary event the detection team owns. The output is a commit to the detection repo, an updated IR playbook, a signed bundle for audit. That framing is closer to how detection engineers already work - they ship rules, they don't manage dashboards.

ONE-LINER

"The instrument for cloud detection engineering. Run real APT chains in your own AWS. Take home the Sigma rules, the evidence, and the playbook."

The five first principles

  1. APT-led, not abstract. Every chain names a real adversary: Scattered Spider, AMBERSQUID, BlackCat, TeamTNT, SCARLETEED, LAPSUS$. Generic "Persistence test #3" never enters the catalogue.
  2. Three deliverables, not one. Every run ships Sigma rules and an IR playbook and a signed evidence bundle. Detection vendors author rules; SOAR vendors author response; we author both, generated from the same emulation. Nobody else closes that loop.
  3. Signed bundle, portable. Every run produces a hash-chained evidence packet replayable offline against any stack. The bundle is the deliverable.
  4. Open core, Apache 2.0 engine. OSS keeps the editorial standard honest and pulls top-of-funnel. Hosted captures the value (workspace, signer, adapters, support).
  5. AWS-first, depth before breadth. Azure and GCP are roadmap, not v1. We win because nobody has gone this deep on AWS attack chains; spreading thin would lose the moat.

What we are not

  • Not BAS. BAS is enterprise, agent-based, host-shaped, six-figure contracts. We are bottoms-up, cloud-native, detection-engineer-led.
  • Not CSPM. Wiz, Prisma, Lacework find misconfigurations. We test behavior. Adjacent, not overlapping.
  • Not a pentest tool. Pacu and CloudGoat are pentest-flavored. We are detection-engineer-flavored. Same target, different buyer.
  • Not a CTF. CloudGoat ships vulnerable labs to break into. We ship attack chains to test what your stack sees.
  • Not SOAR. Tines, Torq, Splunk SOAR, Palo Alto XSOAR orchestrate response workflows you've already written. We author the playbook from a real run; they execute it. Complementary, not competing.
02 The wedge ICP: cloud-native detection engineers.

The buyer who feels the pain hardest, has the budget authority, and has the technical capacity to integrate the output. We narrow before we broaden.

Primary ICP: detection-eng. teams at cloud-native scale-ups

SHAPE

Series B-D cloud-native company

200-2,000 employees, 80%+ workloads on AWS, dedicated security team of 5-15, named detection engineering function with at least one full-time engineer.

BUYER

Head of Detection Engineering / Senior Detection Engineer

Owns the SIEM ruleset. Has budget authority for $20-100k tools without exec sign-off. Hands-on technically; ships rules to production weekly.

CHAMPION

Cloud security engineer or SOC lead

Person who has been begging detection eng to test their rules end-to-end. Knows the gap intimately. Will run the OSS in their AWS dev account before booking a call.

VERTICAL TILT

Fintech · Healthtech · SaaS infra

Regulated enough to need evidence; cloud-native enough to actually have the gap; budget enough to buy hosted. Avoid early-stage startups (no team), enterprise (too slow).

Secondary ICP: MDR / MSSP providers

Managed Detection & Response shops sell cloud detection content to their downstream customers. MayaTrail becomes their R&D engine: every run produces rule content they can fold into their multi-tenant platform. Higher contract value, longer sales cycle. Pursue once primary ICP is closing on its own.

Anti-ICP

  • Pre-series-B startups. No detection team. No buyer.
  • Multi-cloud / on-prem heavy enterprises. Need Azure + GCP + on-prem; we are AWS-first.
  • Compliance-driven buyers who only want a checkbox. Will not value the open-core or community angle; will price-shop against Mitigant. Walk away.
  • Pure-pentest teams. Different workflow; will reach for Pacu. Not our wedge.
PERSONA RULE

If the buyer asks "do you have an Azure version" in the first call, we are not the right tool today. Polite no, stay in touch, revisit in 12 months.

Three founders, OSS already shipping, design partners interested, no immediate cash pressure. We have optionality: bootstrap to first $250-500k ARR, then decide if seed accelerates anything we cannot do alone.

Founder-led design partners

Phase 1 (month 3-9): Decision point

PHASE 1
Bootstrap or raise
M3 - M9

Two paths fork here. We pick based on signal.

PATH A · CONTINUE BOOTSTRAPPING

If we can hit $25-40k MRR from inbound + light outbound at 30%+ gross margin by month 9, we keep going. Profitable, slow-and-steady, full ownership. Hire engineer #2 from revenue.

PATH B · RAISE SEED ($2-3M)

If we cannot make inbound + outbound work, OR if we see a market window closing (Mitigant raising B, AWS launching first-party, DataDog acquiring Stratus), we raise. The seed buys: dedicated GTM hire, marketing budget for 2 conferences, multi-cloud expansion ahead of competitor.

The trigger: if 3+ of the following are true at month 6, we raise. Otherwise we stay bootstrapped.

  • Inbound below 5 qualified conversations/month
  • Mitigant raises Series B at $50M+ post
  • A second well-funded competitor enters cloud-native
  • Customer asks for Azure/GCP from >30% of conversations
  • OSS contributor growth flatlines under 50/month

Phase 2 (month 9-18): Scale the loop that worked

PHASE 2
Scale on the winning path

Goal: 20-30 paying teams. $50-100k MRR. Defendable product moat (15+ APT chains, 50+ Sigma rules contributed back, Sentinel + Splunk + Elastic adapters shipping).

05 Pricing & packaging: open core.

The OSS is the moat. The hosted tier is the business. The pricing must respect both - free has to be genuinely free, paid has to be obviously worth it.

The two tiers

Tier Includes Price
MayaTrail Core
APACHE 2.0 · SELF-HOSTED		 Engine + 6 APT chains + 11+ Sigma rules + Pulumi sandboxes + CLI + MITRE map + IR playbook templates. Run anywhere. Byte-identical evidence bundles. Free · forever
MayaTrail Platform
HOSTED · TEAM		 Everything in Core + hosted control plane, evidence signer, SIEM adapters (Sentinel, Splunk, Chronicle, Elastic, Wazuh), shared workspace, audit log, SSO, priority new-APT chains. $1.2k / mo · team starter
MayaTrail Enterprise
HOSTED · ANNUAL		 Everything in Platform + custom chain authoring, dedicated APT research, on-prem control plane option, premium support, contractual SLAs, training. From $36k / year

Why these numbers

  • $1.2k/mo · team starter. Below the threshold of needing exec approval. A senior detection engineer can expense it. Equivalent to a single conference ticket per quarter.
  • $36k/year · enterprise floor. Three seats × $12k loaded. Equivalent to a single mid-tier SIEM subscription. Affordable to series-C and above without procurement gymnastics.
  • No per-run pricing. We refuse meter-based pricing because it discourages the behavior we want (running drills frequently). Unlimited runs at all tiers.
  • No per-seat for Platform. Team starter includes up to 5 engineers. Beyond that, enterprise. Removes friction for adoption inside teams.

What we won't sell

  • One-time perpetual licenses. Doesn't fit the subscription model; doesn't justify ongoing APT chain investment.
  • Pure free for everyone. A free tier inside Platform encourages buyers to never upgrade. Free is OSS; Platform is paid.
  • Heavy discounting. Detection engineers respect price discipline; aggressive discounting signals desperation. We hold the line.
PRICING DISCIPLINE

If a prospect asks for a 50% discount, we say no and walk. The goal is 30+ teams at full price, not 200 teams at half price - the operational load of half-paid customers will sink us.

06 Sales motion: founder-led through deal #25.

No SDRs, no AEs, no marketing-qualified-lead funnel until we hit deal 25. The founders close every contract personally. The reason: detection engineers can tell when they are being sold to, and they buy from peers.

The conversation flow

  1. First touch: technical. Either inbound from OSS / talk / newsletter, or outbound with a specific Sigma rule we wrote for their stack. Never "would you like a demo of our platform" - always "here's a rule you might want, want to talk about how we generated it".
  2. Demo: 30 minutes, founder-led. Always start with their pain - "what do you currently do to test your cloud detections?" Their answer becomes the demo flow. Mirror, don't pitch.
  3. POC: 14-day, self-serve. They get Platform access, we ship one custom APT chain for their stack. They run it in their own AWS, against their own SIEM. Evidence bundle includes rules that fire AND rules they should write.
  4. Close: contract over Notion / DocuSign. Single-page MSA. Annual prepay or monthly. No legal back-and-forth for <$50k deals.
  5. Onboard: founder-led for first 15 customers. Slack channel, weekly check-in for first month, monthly product council. Customer success comes after deal 25.

Objections we expect, by frequency

Objection Response
"We already use Mitigant" Different framing. Mitigant integrates with your SIEM; we generate detection content as output. Ask if their team writes their own Sigma rules. If yes, we are the upstream.
"We use Stratus Red Team" Great tool for atomic tests. APT chains across services are what we ship. Want to see the Scattered Spider chain?
"Why not just write the rules ourselves?" You can - and most do. Our value is the run that proves the rules fire end-to-end, plus the evidence bundle. Even an experienced detection engineer takes 2-3 days per APT chain; we ship one in an hour.
"We need Azure too" Honest answer: not yet. AWS-first. Azure roadmap in 2027 if AWS depth lets us. Stay in touch.
"Is this safe for production?" It never touches production. Sandbox account, STS-only role, Pulumi sandbox with one-command destroy + post-conditions. Demo the safety model first.
"We need on-prem" Self-host the OSS core. Same engine, same bundle output. Enterprise tier offers on-prem control plane if needed.
"Can we pay quarterly / monthly?" Yes for Platform. Enterprise is annual.
FOUNDER-LED HEURISTIC

If you find yourself answering a question you've answered three times before, write a doc, link it, move on. Never answer the same question a fifth time live - that's the signal to write content.

07 Content & community: credibility, not lead-gen.

Every piece of content we publish should pass a single test: would I read this if I were the audience? If the answer is no, don't ship it. Detection engineers smell marketing copy from orbit and respect technical depth.

What we publish

CHAIN POSTS

How we built X

2,000+ words per APT. The boto3, the IAM, the Sigma rule. One per chain shipped. SEO target: "AMBERSQUID emulation", "AWS attack simulation".

TECHNIQUE NOTES

One rule, dissected

Short pieces (500-800 words) on a single Sigma rule. How it works, what it catches, what evades it. Aimed at SigmaHQ-level credibility.

ANNUAL REPORT

State of cloud detection

Yearly long-form, January release. Coverage gaps, what fires fastest, what doesn't. Becomes the artifact buyers cite for "why we need this".

Community plays

  • Open detection challenge (quarterly). We publish a 5-step AWS chain via blog + Twitter. First detection engineer to send us a working Sigma rule gets a $500 prize + their rule in our OSS. Marketing as an editorial event.
  • Cloud Detection Eng. office hours (monthly). Open 1-hour Zoom. Anyone can show up with a rule they're stuck on. Founders + 1-2 community members debug live.
  • Sponsor (small) of community events we already love. BSides Bangalore, fwd:cloudsec. $1-2k each. Pure brand investment.
  • Co-author with respected detection engineers. Once we have visibility, invite folks like Florian Roth (Sigma), Christophe Tafani-Dereeper (Stratus author at DataDog) to collaborate on a chain. Position as peers.
CREDIBILITY RULE

Our community work is given freely. We do not gate content behind email forms. We do not put "request a demo" between the reader and a useful Sigma rule. The reward for being useful is being remembered.

08 OSS strategy: genuine, with a clear monetization line.

Open core only works when both halves are honest: the OSS has to be a tool people would use even if the company disappeared, and the paid tier has to add capabilities that genuinely require us. Bad open core (the "tease and switch" model) corrodes trust faster than any other go-to-market sin.

What's in OSS (Apache 2.0, forever)

  • Engine: chain orchestrator, Pulumi templates, MITRE mapper, evidence emitter
  • All 6 APT chains we currently ship (and every future chain - we never paywall a chain)
  • 11 Sigma rules + future rules - never paywall content content
  • CLI: mayatrail run <chain>, full functionality
  • IR playbook templates, MITRE coverage map generator

What's in Platform (paid)

  • Hosted control plane (run history, scheduling, team workspace)
  • Evidence signer (cryptographic + key management)
  • SIEM adapters with live ingest (Sentinel, Splunk, Chronicle, Elastic, Wazuh)
  • Custom chain authoring UI
  • SSO, audit log, role-based access
  • Priority access to new APT chains 30 days before OSS release
  • Support, SLAs, training

The rules we hold ourselves to

  1. No chain or rule is ever paywalled forever. 30-day exclusivity for Platform is acceptable. Permanent paywall is not.
  2. If we deprecate an OSS feature, we replace it. Functionality only moves OSS → Paid if there's a real reason (security, compute cost). And we ship a replacement.
  3. License is and stays Apache 2.0. We don't BSL or SSPL ourselves. If competitors fork, we compete on velocity + community + hosted, not lawyers.
  4. Contributors get credit, always. Every community-contributed chain is named after its author in the catalogue.
  5. Public roadmap, public issues, public design discussions. The OSS is the company's nervous system.
PRECEDENTS

HashiCorp's BSL move cost them more goodwill than it bought revenue. Elastic's SSPL did the same. dbt has held the line and won. We model on dbt: paid features are real value-add, OSS stays Apache 2.0.
